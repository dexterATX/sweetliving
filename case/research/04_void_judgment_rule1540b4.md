# Vacating a void Florida judgment — Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.540(b)(4) (current law), service/default defects in residential eviction, and county-court jurisdiction to rule while the 2DCA appeal is pending
SUMMARY: Rule 1.540(b)(4) is Rodgers' correct vehicle and it has no 1-year limit: the current rule text (verified from the official April 1, 2026 consolidated edition) applies the 1-year cap only to grounds (1)-(3), leaving void-judgment motions subject only to a 'reasonable time' standard — though the motion does not stay the writ or affect finality. To be VOID (not merely voidable) under controlling law (Curbelo v. Ullman, Fla. 1990; Sterling Factors, 2d DCA 2007 — her district), the judgment must rest on lack of subject-matter jurisdiction, lack of personal jurisdiction from absent/defective service, or an outright due-process deprivation of notice and opportunity to be heard (Shiver v. Wharton; Torres v. One Stop). Her two strongest void theories: (1) defective service (the actual method per the docket was substitute service under § 48.031(1)(a) on her 15-year-old son at 3929 Argyle St. — an address that was never her usual place of abode — through a closed storm door; § 48.183 posting rules also inform the analysis; strict compliance is jurisdictional per Shurman); and (2) defaults and default final judgments entered despite her filed answers and motions — categorically improper under current Rule 1.500(a)-(e) (rewritten EFFECTIVE JAN. 1, 2026 per SC2022-0575, requiring a filed-and-served motion for default in every case), under Picchi (Fla. 1988), and under the Stuart-Findlay/Motor Sport line requiring vacatur of post-answer defaults regardless of neglect, merit, or time. On the appeal interplay: FILE the 1.540(b)(4) motion in Pasco county court now, but under Fla. R. App. P. 9.600(b) and the Duryea (2d DCA)/Herbits/Weeks divestiture line the county court cannot grant relief until the 2DCA relinquishes jurisdiction — file a 9.600(b) motion to relinquish in 2D2026-1666 the same week. A denial is separately appealable under Rule 9.130(a)(5) within 30 days. The 'never-a-tenant/purchaser' theory is powerful on the merits and on direct appeal but is a low-confidence basis for the 'void' label.

## Current text of Rule 1.540(b) — no 1-year limit for void-judgment motions
- AUTHORITY: Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.540(b) (official consolidated text, April 1, 2026 edition) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: "On motion and upon such terms as are just, the court may relieve a party... from a final judgment... for the following reasons: (1) mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect; (2) newly discovered evidence...; (3) fraud...; (4) that the judgment, decree, or order is void; or (5) ...satisfied, released, or discharged.... The motion shall be filed within a reasonable time, and for reasons (1), (2), and (3) not more than 1 year after the judgment... was entered or taken." The 1-year cap applies ONLY to grounds (1)-(3); a truly void judgment may be attacked at any time.
- APPLIES: The (b)(4) motion is not time-barred; still file promptly (delay invites laches arguments).
- DEADLINE: (b)(4): 'reasonable time' — file ASAP. Alternative (b)(1) grounds expire ~June 2027 (1 year from judgment).
- ACTIONS: Caption it 'Verified Motion to Vacate Void Judgment,' cite 1.540(b)(4) as the primary ground; quote the timing sentence to preempt objections.
- URL: https://www-media.floridabar.org/uploads/2026/04/Civil-Procedure-Rules-04-01-26.pdf
- CONFIDENCE: high

## A 1.540(b) motion does NOT stay the judgment or the writ
- AUTHORITY: Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.540(b), post-enumeration paragraph (April 1, 2026 edition) [CORRECTED — the sentence is verbatim but sits in the timing paragraph, not the rule's final paragraph]
- SAYS: "A motion under this subdivision does not affect the finality of a judgment, decree, or order or suspend its operation."
- APPLIES: The motion will not undo the July 9 writ execution or toll any appellate deadline. Stay relief is separate (Rule 9.310).
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Void vs. voidable — the controlling Florida Supreme Court test
- AUTHORITY: Curbelo v. Ullman, 571 So. 2d 443, 445 (Fla. 1990) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: Where a court is legally organized, has subject-matter jurisdiction, and the adverse parties are given an opportunity to be heard, errors and irregularities — short of an illegal deprivation of the opportunity to be heard — do not render the judgment void.
- APPLIES: Build the (b)(4) motion on the three recognized void categories: (1) no subject-matter jurisdiction, (2) no personal jurisdiction (defective/absent service), (3) complete due-process deprivation. Plead the service defects and the notice-free dispositive steps as the void grounds; plead the default-despite-answers error both as reinforcing due process and as independent Rule 1.500 error.
- URL: https://law.justia.com/cases/florida/supreme-court/1990/75125-0.html
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Second DCA's own void/voidable framework (controlling in her district)
- AUTHORITY: Sterling Factors Corp. v. U.S. Bank Nat'l Ass'n, 968 So. 2d 658, 665 (Fla. 2d DCA 2007) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: "A void judgment is so defective that it is deemed never to have had legal force and effect"; lack of subject-matter jurisdiction makes a judgment void; "[a] judgment that is entered against a defendant when the court has no personal jurisdiction over the defendant is generally regarded as a void judgment." BUT where the movant was properly served, participated, and merely lacked actual notice of one hearing, the judgment was 'at best, voidable.'
- APPLIES: Because Rodgers appeared at hearings (Mar 24, Jun 2 (late), Jun 18), a generic no-notice theory meets Sterling Factors resistance. Lead with the service-of-process defects (personal jurisdiction), and isolate exactly which dispositive step occurred without any notice despite her filed papers.
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Due-process deprivation renders a judgment void — supporting DCA line
- AUTHORITY: Shiver v. Wharton, 9 So. 3d 687, 690 (Fla. 4th DCA 2009); Torres v. One Stop Maint. & Mgmt., Inc., 178 So. 3d 86, 89 (Fla. 4th DCA 2015) [CORRECTED — quotes verbatim; two add-on propositions need their own citations: 'no discretion — obligated to vacate' is Vercosa v. Fields, 174 So. 3d 550, 552 (Fla. 4th DCA 2015); relief-not-pled due-process rule is Daniels v. Sorriso Dental Studio, LLC, 164 So. 3d 778 (Fla. 2d DCA 2015)]
- SAYS: Shiver: a judgment is void if the proceedings leading to it violated "the due process guarantee of notice and an opportunity to be heard." Torres: "A judgment entered without such notice and opportunity to be heard is void." Vercosa: where a judgment is void the trial court "has no discretion, but is obligated to vacate the judgment."
- APPLIES: Pair with Curbelo: default judgment entered without notice while her answers and motions were on file = 'illegal deprivation of the opportunity to be heard.' (4th DCA cases are persuasive in the 2d DCA; square them with Sterling Factors.)
- CONFIDENCE: medium

## Service defects: total absence of valid service = void; defective-but-notice-giving service = voidable
- AUTHORITY: Veigle v. St. Cloud Marine, Inc., 818 So. 2d 695, 696 (Fla. 5th DCA 2002); Shurman v. Atlantic Mortg. & Inv. Corp., 795 So. 2d 952, 954 (Fla. 2001) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: Veigle distinguishes total want of service (void) from irregular service that actually gave notice (voidable). Shurman: service statutes are strictly construed and enforced 'because of the importance of litigants receiving notice of actions against them.'
- APPLIES: Per the docket documents, service was SUBSTITUTE SERVICE (§ 48.031(1)(a)) on her 15-year-old son at 3929 Argyle St. on May 14, 2026 — valid only at the defendant's 'usual place of abode' with a co-resident 15+. The Argyle address was never her (or his) usual place of abode, the return shows papers left through a closed storm door, and the 'informing' requirement was unmet. Anticipate the Veigle counter (she clearly knew of the case); rebut with Shurman strict compliance and the fact that her Motion to Quash (DIN 27, May 21; renewed DIN 36, June 3) PRESERVED the insufficiency defense — and was never ruled on (swept into DIN 44's 'abandoned' finding). No Rule 1.140(h) waiver problem: the defense was raised.
- ACTIONS: Get the return of service (DIN 23) and compare against § 48.031(1)(a); attach the unruled quash motions as exhibits.
- URL: https://caselaw.findlaw.com/court/fl-supreme-court/1219110.html
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Fla. Stat. § 48.183 (2025) — posting-service requirements checklist (context)
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 48.183(1)-(2) (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: Posting in a residential possession action ('including those under chapters 83, 723, and 513') is allowed only if the tenant cannot be found and no person 15+ resides at the tenant's usual place of abode, after at least two service attempts ≥ 6 hours apart. If service is solely by posting, the clerk must mail a copy (landlord furnishes copy + prestamped envelope), note the mailing on the docket, and file a certificate; service is effective on the later of posting or mailing; at least 5 days must elapse before a removal judgment.
- APPLIES: Here the return shows substitute service rather than posting, but § 48.183's premises-focused regime underscores the core defect: all service ran to an address she never occupied. If any posting-only service occurred at any stage, the checklist defects (attempts/spacing, clerk mailing certificate, 5-day minimum, wrong 'usual place of abode') are each provable from the file. Note: posting-only service cannot support a money judgment.
- URL: http://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0000-0099/0048/Sections/0048.183.html
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Rule 1.500 defaults — CURRENT text (rewritten effective Jan. 1, 2026)
- AUTHORITY: Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.500(a)-(e) (April 1, 2026 edition); In re Amends. to Fla. R. Civ. P. 1.440 & 1.500, No. SC2022-0575 (Fla. Nov. 13, 2025) [CORRECTED — rewrite effective Jan. 1, 2026, not 2025]
- SAYS: (a) clerk default only where the party 'has failed to file or serve any document in the action'; (b) court default requires the movant to 'file and serve a motion for default'; (c) 'Until a default is entered against a party, all documents filed in the action or otherwise required to be served must be served on that party. A party may plead or otherwise defend at any time before default is entered.'; (d) defaults set aside per 1.540(b); (e) 'notice to the defaulted party of any evidentiary hearing' and any trial before final judgment after default.
- APPLIES: She had filed answers, motions, and a jury demand: (i) a clerk default was categorically unavailable; (ii) a court default required a filed-and-served motion (check service of the June 1 renewed motion — it was mailed June 1 for a June 2 hearing); (iii) she was entitled to plead or otherwise defend at any time before default — she already had; (iv) she was entitled to notice of any evidentiary hearing preceding the default judgments. The June 3 and June 18 'judicial defaults' over filed responsive papers violate every provision. Quote the CURRENT text; cite Picchi for the principle and note the 2026 rewrite strengthened it.
- URL: https://www-media.floridabar.org/uploads/2026/04/Civil-Procedure-Rules-04-01-26.pdf (pp. 167-68)
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Picchi — notice required where defendant has filed any paper
- AUTHORITY: Picchi v. Barnett Bank of S. Fla., N.A., 521 So. 2d 1090 (Fla. 1988) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: Even a bare notice of appearance triggers the right to notice of the default application; only the court (not the clerk) may default a party who has filed any paper.
- APPLIES: A fortiori: she filed answers, a verified answer, a quash motion, a rent-determination motion, and a jury demand.
- URL: http://library.law.fsu.edu/Digital-Collections/flsupct/dockets/70464/op-70464.pdf
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Default entered after a responsive filing must be vacated — regardless of neglect, merit, or time
- AUTHORITY: Stuart-Findlay v. Bank of Am., N.A., 183 So. 3d 468 (Fla. 4th DCA 2016) [CONFIRMED]; Motor Sport Eng'g, Inc. v. Car Point, Inc., 611 So. 2d 15 (Fla. 3d DCA 1992) [CONFIRMED]; Int'l Energy Corp. v. Hackett, 687 So. 2d 941 (Fla. 3d DCA 1997) [CORRECTED — actual sentence: 'any default entered in violation of the due process notice requirement of Rule 1.500 must be set aside without any regard as to whether a meritorious defense is presented or excusable neglect is established']; Crocker Invs., Inc. v. Statesman Life Ins. Co., 515 So. 2d 1305 (Fla. 3d DCA 1987) [CORRECTED — supports vacatur on a successive motion via res judicata flexibility; cautions the notice error 'does not usually render the judgment void']
- SAYS: A default entered after the defendant filed/served responsive papers is erroneous as a matter of law; no excusable-neglect/meritorious-defense/due-diligence showing is required (Stuart-Findlay); a response 'manifesting an intent to defend on the merits' bars default (Motor Sport).
- APPLIES: The cleanest merits path: her answers and motions predated both defaults, so the defaults were erroneous as a matter of law and the judgments built on them must fall. Plead in the alternative with (b)(4) so relief is available even if labeled 'voidable'; the (b)(1) alternative stays timely to ~June 2027.
- CONFIDENCE: medium

## Rule 9.600 — lower tribunal's jurisdiction during the appeal
- AUTHORITY: Fla. R. App. P. 9.600(a), (b) (July 1, 2026 edition) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: (b): 'If the jurisdiction of the lower tribunal has been divested by an appeal from a final order, the court by order may permit the lower tribunal to proceed with specifically stated matters during the pendency of the appeal.'
- APPLIES: The appeal divested the county court of jurisdiction over the judgment. The 9.600(b) motion in the 2DCA is the mechanism to let the county court hear and DECIDE the 1.540(b)(4) motion. Do not rely on (a)'s narrow concurrent-jurisdiction window — a 1.540 merits ruling is not a mere procedural matter.
- ACTIONS: File in 2D2026-1666 a 'Motion to Relinquish Jurisdiction to the County Court' attaching the filed 1.540(b)(4) motion; suggest 60–90 days; note that vacatur below could moot the appeal and conserve the court's resources.
- CONFIDENCE: high

## FILE below now; do not let the county court rule before relinquishment
- AUTHORITY: Duryea v. Bono, 249 So. 3d 780, 781 (Fla. 2d DCA 2018) [CONFIRMED]; Herbits v. City of Miami, 197 So. 3d 575, 579 (Fla. 3d DCA 2016) [CONFIRMED for divestiture/nullity; its pending motion was mandamus, not 1.540]; Weeks v. Universal Prop. & Cas. Ins. Co., No. 5D2023-0945 (Fla. 5th DCA Oct. 8, 2024) [CONFIRMED verbatim] [CORRECTED — the 9.600(b)-for-1.540 framing is a synthesis, not a quotable Herbits holding]
- SAYS: Filing the notice of appeal divests the trial court of jurisdiction to rule on a pending motion to vacate (Weeks, citing Duryea and Herbits); a post-notice order modifying the judgment 'is a nullity' (Duryea — 2d DCA, controlling in Pasco).
- APPLIES: (1) FILE the verified 1.540(b)(4) motion in county court now (starts the 'reasonable time' clock; relinquishment is usually conditioned on a filed motion); (2) move the 2DCA under 9.600(b) the same week; (3) if a hearing is set below before relinquishment, alert the court to the jurisdictional issue rather than risk a nullity order. If the appeal ever ends or is dismissed, county-court jurisdiction returns automatically.
- CONFIDENCE: medium

## An order denying the 1.540(b) motion is itself appealable
- AUTHORITY: Fla. R. App. P. 9.130(a)(5) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: Orders on authorized, timely 1.540 motions are reviewable by appeal; 30 days from rendition; rehearing does not toll.
- APPLIES: A denial gets its own fresh 30-day appeal window, independent of 2D2026-1666.
- CONFIDENCE: medium

## Subject-matter-jurisdiction / wrong-remedy theory — use with caution
