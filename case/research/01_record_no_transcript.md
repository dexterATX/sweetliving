# Reconstructing the appellate record when no transcript exists — Fla. R. App. P. 9.200 (current July 1, 2026 rules), 2D DCA Case No. 2D2026-1666
SUMMARY: Under the current Florida Rules of Appellate Procedure (official compilation updated July 1, 2026), the no-transcript reconstruction tool is confirmed at Rule 9.200(b)(5) 'Statement of Evidence or Proceedings': a party may prepare a statement from the best available means including her own recollection, must serve it on all other parties (15-day window for objections/proposed amendments — not the 10 days shown in older versions), then file it with the lower tribunal for settlement and approval, after which the clerk includes it in the record. Contrary to a common assumption imported from federal practice, the current Florida rule contains NO express deadline tied to the initial brief; the practical deadlines are the record transmission (60 days from the Jun 24 notice → ~Aug 24, 2026) and the initial brief (70 days → served by Sep 2, 2026), with the answer brief 30 days later and reply 30 days after that. Rodgers's Jul 3 designation and directions to the clerk were timely (deadline Jul 6 after the weekend roll). Without a settled statement, Applegate v. Barnett Bank, 377 So. 2d 1150, 1152 (Fla. 1979) dictates affirmance of any issue that depends on unrecorded hearing events, because the judgment is presumed correct and the appellant bears the burden of demonstrating error on the record; the (b)(5) statement is the recognized cure, and 9.200(f)(2) guarantees an opportunity to supplement before the appeal is decided on an incomplete record — but (f) cannot manufacture a never-made transcript, and a statement never actually approved by the trial judge is worthless. Immediate action sequence: serve draft statements for the Mar 24, Jun 2, and Jun 18 hearings on opposing counsel this week; file the county-court settlement package after the 15-day objection window; frame lead appellate issues as face-of-the-record legal errors (default entered over a filed verified answer; voidness) that need no transcript; and if settlement runs long, file a Rule 9.300 extension motion in the 2DCA before Sep 2 with a specific proposed due date and a detailed certificate of conferral (a first stipulated/unopposed request is routinely granted per the 2DCA's Practice Preferences; the motion itself tolls the deadline). One overriding caution: the pending 2DCA show-cause proceeding and the Rule 2.330 disqualification motion can each change who settles the statement and whether the default schedule even applies — read the Jun 26 and Jul 7 orders in ACIS first.

## Statement of the evidence or proceedings when no transcript exists — confirmed at Rule 9.200(b)(5) in the current rules
- AUTHORITY: Fla. R. App. P. 9.200(b)(5) (Florida Rules of Appellate Procedure, official compilation updated July 1, 2026) [verified against official compilation]
- SAYS: The provision is now titled '(5) Statement of Evidence or Proceedings' and sits at subdivision (b)(5) of Rule 9.200 (verified in the July 1, 2026 official text). Verbatim: 'If no report of the proceedings was made, or if the transcript is unavailable, a party may prepare a statement of the evidence or proceedings from the best available means, including the party's recollection. The statement must be served on all other parties, who may serve objections or proposed amendments to it within 15 days of service. Thereafter, the statement and any objections or proposed amendments must be filed with the lower tribunal for settlement and approval. As settled and approved, the statement must be included by the clerk of the lower tribunal in the record.' IMPORTANT CORRECTION TO A COMMON ASSUMPTION: the CURRENT Florida rule contains NO express deadline tied to the initial brief (that 14-day deadline exists in federal FRAP 10(c), not the Florida rule; older secondary sources also mention a now-obsolete '110 days' figure and a 10-day objection window from prior rule versions — the current objection window is 15 days). The practical deadline is driven by the record-transmission and briefing schedule (Rules 9.110(e) and (f)).

## Correcting and supplementing the record
- AUTHORITY: Fla. R. App. P. 9.200(f)(1)-(2) (July 1, 2026) [verified verbatim per verifier]
- SAYS: Verbatim: '(1) If there is an error or omission in the record, the parties by stipulation, the lower tribunal before the record is transmitted, or the court may correct the record. (2) If the court finds the record is incomplete, it must direct a party to supply the omitted parts of the record. No proceeding will be determined, because of an incomplete record, until an opportunity to supplement the record has been given.' The 1977 committee note explains the rule 'is intended to ensure that appellate proceedings will be decided on their merits' with 'no showing of good cause, negligence, or accident ... required before the lower tribunal or the court orders the completion of the record,' but cautions: 'The rule is not intended to cure inadequacies in the record that result from the failure of a party to make a proper record during the proceedings in the lower tribunal.'
- APPLIES: Two uses for Rodgers: (1) before the record transmits (~Aug 24), the county court can correct/add items; after transmission, she moves in the 2DCA to supplement — this is the vehicle to add a later-settled 9.200(b)(5) statement if settlement finishes after transmission; (2) the (f)(2) sentence is a safeguard: the 2DCA must give an opportunity to supplement before deciding the appeal on an incomplete record. Caveat: (f) cures omissions of things that EXIST; it does not manufacture a transcript that was never made — that gap is filled only by the (b)(5) statement or an (a)(3) stipulated statement.
- DEADLINE: Stipulation or lower-tribunal correction available until record transmission (~Aug 24, 2026); motion to supplement in the 2DCA available any time before decision

## Consequences of an inadequate record — Applegate affirmance rule and how a 9.200(b)(5) statement mitigates it
- AUTHORITY: Applegate v. Barnett Bank of Tallahassee, 377 So. 2d 1150, 1152 (Fla. 1979) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: Operative language (verified against multiple reporters of the opinion): 'In appellate proceedings the decision of a trial court has the presumption of correctness and the burden is on the appellant to demonstrate error.' 'Without a record of the trial proceedings, the appellate court can not properly resolve the underlying factual issues so as to conclude that the trial court's judgment is not supported by the evidence or by an alternative theory.' The Court held the trial court should have been affirmed 'because the record brought forward by the appellant is inadequate to demonstrate reversible error.' Also states the 'tipsy coachman' principle: 'even when based on erroneous reasoning, a conclusion or decision of a trial court will generally be affirmed if the evidence or an alternative theory supports it.' Practical gloss from The Florida Bar Journal: a (b)(5) statement must show the factual context as presented to the lower court; the appellee has a duty to assist in preparing the record and cannot ignore the submission and object later; but a statement merely filed with the clerk WITHOUT actual trial-court approval leaves the record insufficient and results in affirmance; if the trial judge cannot recall or settle disputes, the evidence is deemed incomplete and the judgment is affirmed. DCAs have relinquished jurisdiction to allow settlement of a statement.
- APPLIES: This is the single biggest structural threat to Rodgers's appeal: with no transcript of the Mar 24, Jun 2, or Jun 18 hearings, any argument that depends on what happened AT the hearings will draw an Applegate affirmance unless she gets a settled-and-approved 9.200(b)(5) statement into the record. Two mitigating notes: (1) a properly settled (b)(5) statement is the recognized cure Applegate itself contemplates; (2) issues on the FACE of the written record — default entered despite filed responsive papers, lack of jurisdiction, voidness under Rule 1.540(b)(4) — are pure questions of law reviewable de novo without any transcript, so the strongest appellate issues should be framed as face-of-the-record/legal errors wherever honestly possible.
- DEADLINE: none directly, but drives the urgency of the 9.200(b)(5) process before briefing (~Sep 2, 2026)
- ACTIONS: Structure the initial brief so the lead issues (default entered over a filed answer; void judgment; jurisdiction) rely only on filed documents and orders; Pursue the 9.200(b)(5) statement in parallel for any issue that requires what was said/shown at the hearings
- URL: https://law.justia.com/cases/florida/supreme-court/1979/55345-0.html (practice gloss: https://www.floridabar.org/the-florida-bar-journal/the-record-on-appeal-what-do-you-do-when-there-was-no-court-reporter/)
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Initial brief deadline and brief requirements
- AUTHORITY: Fla. R. App. P. 9.110(f), 9.210(a), (b), (g), and 9.045(b), (e) (July 1, 2026) [verified verbatim per verifier]
- SAYS: 9.110(f) verbatim: 'The appellant's initial brief must be served within 70 days of filing the notice. Additional briefs must be served as prescribed by rule 9.210.' 9.210(g): 'the answer brief must be served within 30 days after service of the initial brief; the reply brief, if any, must be served within 30 days after service of the answer brief' (note: the reply window is now 30 days — older sources say 20). 9.210(a)(2)(B): initial and answer briefs 'must not exceed 13,000 words or 50 pages' and the reply brief 4,000 words or 15 pages; cover sheet, tables, certificates, and signature block are excluded from the count. 9.210(b) requires, in order: table of contents; table of citations; statement of the case and facts 'with references to the appropriate pages of the record or transcript'; summary of argument; argument 'with citation to appropriate authorities, and including the applicable appellate standard of review'; conclusion stating precise relief; certificate of service; certificate of compliance. 9.045(b): computer-generated documents must be in Arial 14-point or Bookman Old Style 14-point, double-spaced, black type. 9.045(e): certificate of compliance 'signed by counsel, or the party if unrepresented.'

Every factual assertion in her statement of the case and facts must cite a record page — which is exactly why the settled 9.200(b)(5) statement must be IN the record before she briefs hearing-dependent facts. As a pro se party she signs her own certificate of compliance. Note also 9.300(b): a timely-served extension motion tolls the briefing schedule until the 2DCA rules.

## Extensions of time in the 2DCA — rule mechanics and local practice for a pro se appellant
- AUTHORITY: Fla. R. App. P. 9.300(a)-(b), (d) (July 1, 2026); 2DCA Practice Preferences (official court publication); 2DCA Admin. Order 2013-1 [quotes verified against official PDFs per verifier]
- SAYS: 9.300(a) verbatim: 'The motion shall state the grounds on which it is based, the relief sought, argument in support thereof, and appropriate citations of authority. A motion for an extension of time shall ... contain a certificate that the movant's counsel has consulted opposing counsel and that the movant's counsel is authorized to represent that opposing counsel either has no objection or will promptly file an objection.' Opposing party may respond within 15 days. 9.300(b): 'service of a motion shall toll the time schedule of any proceeding in the court until disposition of the motion. An order granting an extension of time for any act shall automatically extend the time for all other acts that bear a time relation to it. An order granting an extension of time for preparation of the record ... shall extend automatically, for a like period, the time for service of the next brief.' 9.300(d)(2): motions for stay pending appeal do NOT toll time. 2DCA Practice Preferences: initial extension requests for briefs are often granted (particularly stipulated/unopposed); avoid seriatim requests; under AO 2013-1 parties may file a stipulated notice for a specific extension; a motion must include a specific proposed due date and a detailed certificate of conferral — a generic 'unsuccessful attempt to contact' does not satisfy the requirement.

## Time computation for all the above deadlines
- AUTHORITY: Fla. R. App. P. 9.420(e) (July 1, 2026), incorporating Fla. R. Gen. Prac. & Jud. Admin. 2.514 [CORRECTED by verifier]
- CORRECTION (verified): Rule 9.420(e) verbatim: '(e) Computation. Computation of time is governed by Florida Rule of General Practice and Judicial Administration 2.514.' Current Rule 2.514(a)(1) (post-2024 overhaul, eff. Jan. 1, 2025): '(A) begin counting from the next day that is not a Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday; (B) count every day, including Saturdays, Sundays, and legal holidays that fall in between the first day counted and the last day counted; and (C) include the last day of the period except if the last day is Saturday, Sunday, a legal holiday...then the last day will fall on the next day that is not Saturday, Sunday, a legal holiday.' Day 1 can never fall on a weekend or legal holiday. Rule 2.514(a)(2): for periods of LESS than 7 days, weekends and legal holidays are not counted at all. Rule 2.514(b) adds 5 days after service by mail only.
- APPLIES: The 10-day designation/directions deadline (Jun 24 + 10 = Sat Jul 4) rolled to Mon Jul 6 (her Jul 3 filings were timely); record transmission (Jun 24 + 60 = Sun Aug 23) rolls to Mon Aug 24; the initial brief lands on Wed Sep 2, 2026.
