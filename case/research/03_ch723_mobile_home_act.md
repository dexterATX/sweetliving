# Florida Mobile Home Act (Ch. 723): applicability vs. ch. 83 Part II, exclusive eviction grounds/notice, purchaser rights, ch. 82 unlawful detainer mismatch, and DBPR complaint jurisdiction
SUMMARY: All operative text verified against the 2025 Florida Statutes (current official edition as of July 10, 2026) on leg.state.fl.us/flsenate.gov. Core architecture: § 723.002(1) makes ch. 723 govern when a mobile home OWNER rents only the LOT in a 10+ lot park, while ch. 83 Part II governs only when the home itself is rented — so a ch. 83 'Residential Eviction' against a claimed home PURCHASER with no rental agreement is a vehicle mismatch. § 723.061(1) lets ONLY 'a mobile home park owner' evict, and 'only on' exclusive grounds with fixed notices (5-day demand for lot rent; 7 days after conviction notice; rule-violation tracks; 6 months for change of land use; and, for an unapproved purchaser, 7 days after written notice of failure to be approved, which must be posted AND sent certified/registered mail per § 723.061(4)). § 723.062(1) bars the writ of possession from issuing earlier than 10 days after judgment (vs. ch. 83's 24 hours). Purchaser rights: § 723.059(1) gave Rodgers a right to seek park tenancy that 'may not be unreasonably withheld' and a right to cancel the purchase if approval wasn't granted 5 days before closing; § 723.058 bars park interference with home sales. The strongest structural finding: § 82.02(2) expressly makes ch. 82 (unlawful detainer) INAPPLICABLE 'to the possession of real property under... chapter 723,' and § 82.02(1) excludes ch. 83 tenancies — so against an unapproved purchaser in a ch. 723 park, the park owner's sole remedy was § 723.061(1)(e), which plaintiff (apparently not the park owner) never invoked and whose 7-day notice was apparently never given; and since Rodgers never obtained possession at all, no possession action of any species properly lay against her. Framing caution: § 34.011 gives county courts broad possession jurisdiction, so pitch the mismatch as failure to state a cause of action/standing plus due-process default defects rather than pure subject-matter voidness. DBPR's Division of Florida Condominiums, Timeshares, and Mobile Homes enforces ch. 723 (§§ 723.005-.006) via investigations, cease-and-desist, refund orders, and civil penalties up to $5,000 per violation, with 30/90-day complainant status notices; complaints go on the Mobile Home Complaint Form (2601 Blair Stone Rd, Tallahassee) against the PARK owner, are public records, and DBPR cannot vacate the judgment — if Sweet Living is a dealer rather than the park owner, the sale/title complaint belongs with FLHSMV. Case-law support for exclusivity should be pulled before final briefing; the statutory text carries the argument.

## When ch. 723 applies vs. ch. 83 Part II
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 723.002(1) (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: Ch. 723 applies to "any residential tenancy in which a mobile home is placed upon a rented or leased lot in a mobile home park in which 10 or more lots are offered for rent or lease." When "both the mobile home and lot are rented or when fewer than 10 lots are available for rent or lease, the tenancy shall be governed by the provisions of part II of chapter 83." Once subject to ch. 723, a tenancy stays governed by ch. 723 even if the park later drops below 10 lots.
- APPLIES: The dividing line is WHO owns the home. Rodgers purchased the home for ~$18,000 and never signed any rental agreement for home or lot — on her account there is no ch. 83 tenancy, and any possession dispute concerning a home on a park lot falls in ch. 723 territory (park owner only). The plaintiff's chosen ch. 83 case type presupposes a rental of the dwelling she denies exists. Confirm the Argyle park offers 10+ lots (the DBPR form asks).
- ACTIONS: Establish by affidavit/evidence: 10+ lots; she bought (not rented) the home; no rental agreement exists. Use § 723.002(1) as the framing statute in the 1.540(b) motion and the 2DCA briefing.
- URL: http://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0700-0799/0723/Sections/0723.002.html
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Exclusive grounds and notice periods to evict a mobile home owner from a park
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 723.061(1), (4) (2025); § 723.031(9) (2025) [CORRECTED — verifier fixes integrated below]
- SAYS: § 723.061(1): "A mobile home park owner may evict a mobile home owner, a mobile home tenant, a mobile home occupant, or a mobile home only on one or more of the following grounds:" (a) nonpayment of lot rental — default continuing "for 5 days after delivery of a written demand"; (b) conviction of a violation of law detrimental to health/safety/welfare — vacate within 7 days after notice; (c) rule/agreement/chapter violation — per verifier: (1)(c)1 also authorizes eviction for a FIRST violation found by a court to have "endangered the life, health, safety, or property of the park residents or employees or the peaceful enjoyment of the mobile home park" (7 days to vacate); the (1)(c)2 second-violation track requires written notice within 30 days of the first violation giving 7 days to correct, plus at least 30 days' notice before the vacate date; (d) change in use of land — at least 6 months' notice (with HOA purchase rights); (e) "Failure of the purchaser, prospective tenant, or occupant of a mobile home situated in the mobile home park to be qualified as, and to obtain approval to become, a tenant or occupant of the home... If a purchaser or prospective tenant of a mobile home situated in the mobile home park occupies the mobile home before such approval is granted, the mobile home owner or mobile home tenant must vacate the premises within 7 days after the date the notice of the failure to be approved for tenancy is delivered." § 723.061(4) (per verifier, with its opening clause): "Except for the notice to the officers of the homeowners' association under subparagraph (1)(d)1., any notice required by this section must be in writing, and must be posted on the premises and sent to the mobile home owner and tenant or occupant, as appropriate, by certified or registered mail, return receipt requested"; mailed notice is deemed given 5 days after postmark. § 723.031(9): "No rental agreement shall provide for the eviction of a mobile home owner on a ground other than one contained in s. 723.061."
- APPLIES: Two mismatches: (1) STANDING — only "a mobile home park owner" may evict; if Sweet Living, Inc. is not the park owner it had no ch. 723 eviction right at all. (2) GROUND/NOTICE — the exclusive ground against an unapproved purchaser is (1)(e), requiring the posted + certified-mail 7-day notice; no such statutory notice is known here (the Jan 5 '7-Day Notice' falsely claimed she was residing at the property and was not shown to meet § 723.061(4)). And (1)(e) is triggered only if the purchaser "occupies" the home before approval — Rodgers never took possession.
- ACTIONS: Inspect the court file for ANY pre-suit notice; argue absence of § 723.061 notice as failure of a statutory condition precedent; plead lack of standing.
- URL: http://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0700-0799/0723/Sections/0723.061.html
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Writ of possession timing under ch. 723 (contrast with ch. 83)
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 723.062(1)-(2) (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: § 723.062(1): "...The writ of possession shall not issue earlier than 10 days from the date judgment is granted." § 723.062(2): removal of personal property including the mobile home to or near the property line or into storage; sheriff stand-by at hourly rate; no liability after removal.
- APPLIES: If ch. 723 governed, the writ machinery used was wrong: ch. 83's 24-hour writ vs. ch. 723's 10-day floor. DIN 44 (June 18) honored the 10-day term, but the June 30 order (DIN 55) made the writ issue "immediately" — in tension with § 723.062(1) (though by actual execution on July 9, more than 10 days had elapsed from the June 18 judgment). Concrete illustration of prejudice from the wrong statutory vehicle.
- URL: http://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0700-0799/0723/Sections/0723.062.html
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Retaliatory conduct protections
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 723.0615 (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: Unlawful for a park owner to discriminatorily increase rent, decrease services, or bring/threaten eviction primarily in retaliation for protected conduct (incl. good-faith complaints to governmental agencies); retaliation may be raised as a defense in a possession action; inapplicable where the park owner proves good cause.
- APPLIES: Her planned DBPR/AG/FLHSMV/State Attorney complaints are protected conduct; keep dated proof of every agency complaint to support any future retaliation defense. (The section protects home owners vis-à-vis the PARK owner — weaker fit against Sweet Living as mere seller.)
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Park owner restrictions on resale of homes in the park
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 723.058 (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: A park owner may not deny or abridge a home owner's right to sell; may not require removal of the home based solely on the sale; may not charge a commission/fee unless acting as agent under written contract; may not condition purchaser approval on resale agreements; anti-retaliation at (5).
- APPLIES: If the park obstructed the Potes-to-Rodgers sale or the approval process, that is § 723.058 territory — a DBPR complaint category ("Restrictions on the sale of mobile homes").
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Rights of a PURCHASER of a mobile home situated in a park
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 723.059(1)-(3) (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: (1) The purchaser may become a park tenant subject to park-owner approval, "but such approval may not be unreasonably withheld"; the purchaser may cancel the purchase contract if tenancy approval has not been granted 5 days before closing. (2) Properly promulgated rules may screen prospective purchasers. (3) An approved purchaser may assume the remainder of the seller's rental agreement.
- APPLIES: Rodgers' affirmative framework: a right to seek tenancy not unreasonably withheld, and a statutory right to CANCEL and unwind the $18,000 deal when approval was denied (park manager denied approval Dec 17, 2025; plaintiff's own Dec 31 'Notice of Denial of Tenancy' acknowledged the rescission right; she rescinded by certified mail in Jan 2026). The seller — not Rodgers — created the unapproved-occupancy problem; her remedy sounds in cancellation/restitution, not eviction against her.
- ACTIONS: Document the tenancy application/denial and the timing of approval vs. payment; assert § 723.059(1) cancellation/restitution in any claim against Sweet Living for the $18,000.
- URL: http://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0700-0799/0723/Sections/0723.059.html
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Chapter 83 eviction requires a landlord-tenant relationship; contract-of-sale occupancy is excluded
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 83.43(5), (9), (13), (17) (2025, as renumbered by ch. 2023-8 and ch. 2024-199); § 83.42(2)-(3) (2025) [CORRECTED — current subsection numbers per verifier: 'Landlord' § 83.43(9); 'Tenant' § 83.43(17); 'Rental agreement' § 83.43(13); 'Dwelling unit' § 83.43(5)]
- SAYS: Part II operates only between a "landlord" ("the owner or lessor of a dwelling unit") and a "tenant" ("any person entitled to occupy a dwelling unit under a rental agreement"). § 83.42 excludes from Part II: "(2) Occupancy under a contract of sale of a dwelling unit or the property of which it is a part in which the buyer has paid at least 12 months' rent or in which the buyer has paid at least 1 month's rent and a deposit of at least 5 percent of the purchase price of the property," and "(3) Transient occupancy in... a mobile home park."
- APPLIES: Three layers: (1) no rental agreement → not a "tenant," not a "landlord" — the ch. 83 eviction fails to state a cause of action; (2) if plaintiff characterizes the $18,000 arrangement as contract-of-sale occupancy, § 83.42(2) can exclude it from Part II (fact-dependent thresholds); (3) she never possessed or occupied the premises at all. Frame as failure to state a claim on the face of the record supporting the default judgment, paired with the due-process/notice defects.
- URL: http://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0000-0099/0083/Sections/0083.42.html
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Chapter 82 unlawful detainer — ALSO unavailable for ch. 723 park property
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. §§ 82.01, 82.02(1)-(2), 82.03(1)-(4), 82.04 (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: § 82.03(1): a possession cause of action runs "against a person who obtained possession of that real property by forcible entry, unlawful entry, or unlawful detention." § 82.02 verbatim: "(1) This chapter does not apply to residential tenancies under part II of chapter 83. (2) This chapter does not apply to the possession of real property under chapter 513 or chapter 723." § 82.04: the court generally may not determine title.
- APPLIES: Completes the three-way mismatch: (a) ch. 83 fails for want of a rental agreement; (b) ch. 82 is statutorily inapplicable to ch. 723 park property — and she never "obtained possession" anyway; (c) ch. 723 eviction was available only to the park owner on the (1)(e) notice, which plaintiff is not and did not give.
- URL: https://www.flsenate.gov/Laws/Statutes/2025/82.02
- CONFIDENCE: high

## County court jurisdiction — how to frame the 'voidness' argument
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 34.011 (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: County courts have exclusive jurisdiction of proceedings relating to the right of possession of real property (subject to circuit-court exceptions).
- APPLIES: A pure subject-matter-jurisdiction attack is weak; the wrong CAUSE OF ACTION is reversible error, not automatically void. Lead the 1.540(b)(4) motion with service/notice due-process defects; plead the ch. 723/83/82 mismatch as an independent ground. The title/$18,000 dispute may belong in circuit court — the case as litigated outgrew the eviction vehicle.
- CONFIDENCE: medium

## DBPR division enforcement powers over mobile home parks
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. §§ 723.005, 723.006 (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: The division enforces ch. 723 (except park rules themselves and §§ 723.022, .023, .033): investigations; subpoenas; cease-and-desist orders with affirmative action; refund orders (rent increases, improper fees/charges/assessments); civil penalties up to $5,000 per separate violation ($250 minor); written complainant status notice within 30 days and again within 90 days.
- APPLIES: A DBPR complaint against the PARK OWNER can address unreasonable withholding of purchaser approval (§ 723.059(1)), sale restrictions (§ 723.058), and eviction-related chapter violations. Remedies run to the State — DBPR cannot vacate the judgment or represent her. If Sweet Living is a mobile home DEALER rather than the park owner, the sale/title complaint belongs with FLHSMV (§ 320.77) instead.
- DEADLINE: If filed the week of July 13, 2026: DBPR status notices due ~mid-August and ~mid-October 2026.
- URL: http://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0700-0799/0723/Sections/0723.006.html
- CONFIDENCE: high

## How to file the DBPR mobile home park complaint
- AUTHORITY: DBPR Mobile Home Complaint Form + instructions; Fla. Admin. Code chs. 61B-29 through 61B-35 [CONFIRMED against the form PDF]
- SAYS: Signed form + documentation by mail to Division of Florida Condominiums, Timeshares, and Mobile Homes, 2601 Blair Stone Road, Tallahassee, FL 32399; phone 850-487-9948 / 850-488-1122; online via MyFloridaLicense. The division investigates only ch. 723 / 61B-29–35 violations; routinely investigated categories include "Restrictions on the sale of mobile homes." The complaint and attachments are PUBLIC RECORDS ("the Division cannot protect your identity"). The form asks: lots in park; own home/lease lot; titled as mobile home; whether the park owner was owner of record of a previously owned home; pending court action (case number/county must be disclosed).
- APPLIES: Name the park owner/manager; answer honestly that she purchased a previously owned home in the park and never received title (attach FLHSMV records); disclose case 2026-CC-001256 and appeal 2D2026-1666; attach payment proof and notices. Everything filed is public — keep it word-for-word consistent with sworn court filings.
- URL: https://www2.myfloridalicense.com/lsc/documents/mobilehomecomplaintform.pdf
- CONFIDENCE: high
