# Florida mobile home title law and fraud remedies (Ch. 319 title statutes; criminal/civil fraud remedies; personal-vs-real property status; FLHSMV and State Attorney complaint routes)
SUMMARY: All core statutes verified against the 2025 Florida Statutes on Online Sunshine. (1) § 319.22(1) bars any court from recognizing ownership of a mobile home not evidenced by a duly issued certificate of title — this cuts against Rodgers (she has no title) but equally against Sweet Living if it holds no certificate for the home, making a FLHSMV title-record search (HSMV 90510) the immediate priority. (2) Her fact pattern maps onto three criminal statutes: § 319.34 (selling a mobile home without delivering an assigned certificate — up to $500/6 months per offense), § 319.33 (title fraud — third-degree felony; note the documented $2,000 recorded price, false Jan 2 date, and REBUILT/total-loss brands vs. the seller's no-damage representation — § 319.33(1)(e) false statements), and § 812.014(2)(c)3. — the ~$18,000 payment is grand theft in the THIRD degree ($10,000–$20,000 band), not second degree, which requires $20,000+. (3) Civil remedies: § 772.11 civil theft gives treble damages (~$54,000) and fees but requires a mandatory written pre-suit demand and a 30-day cure window and carries a fee-shifting risk if the claim lacks substantial support; FDUTPA (§§ 501.204, 501.211) gives actual damages plus fees; and a declaratory-judgment/specific-performance action can compel title, which FLHSMV must then honor under § 319.28(2)(a) on an affidavit plus the court order. (4) Because the home sits on a rented park lot, § 320.015 makes it tangible personal property — the eviction judgment could at most decide possession of the lot, never title to the home. (5) Complaint routes: FLHSMV form HSMV 84901 (Mobile Home Dealer complaint) emailed to Region 6 (Tampa; DmvRegion6@flhsmv.gov — covers Pasco); title-fraud reports on HSMV 80122 to the Motor Vehicle Fraud Unit (dmv-enforcement@flhsmv.gov, 850-617-2907); title work at the county tax collector (HSMV 82040 MH / 82050); criminal report to the Pasco Sheriff plus SAO6's Dade City office ((352) 521-4333). Key correction: mobile home DEALER complaints go to FLHSMV, not DBPR — DBPR handles only ch. 723 park-owner violations.

## No marketable title without a certificate; courts may not recognize untitled claims
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 319.22(1) (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: A purchaser "shall not acquire marketable title... until he or she has had issued to him or her a certificate of title"; "no court shall recognize the right, title, claim, or interest of any person in or to any motor vehicle or mobile home sold, disposed of, mortgaged, or encumbered, unless evidenced by a certificate of title duly issued to that person."
- APPLIES: Cuts BOTH ways — and in this case the title is still in seller SALLY POTES' name (per the court record and her verified filings), so it bars SWEET LIVING's claim to the home too. Demand Sweet Living produce a certificate in its name; order the FLHSMV title/lien history (HSMV 90510) before filing the 1.540(b) motion. Caveat: case law recognizes equitable/beneficial ownership between immediate sale parties notwithstanding the statute (chiefly protects third parties) — research before asserting her own ownership in court. NOTE the tension with DIN 44's Finding 1 (court found she 'held valid title' citing Green Tree Acceptance v. Zimerman, 611 So. 2d 608 (Fla. 2d DCA 1993)) — use the finding defensively (she is the owner → ch. 723 governs) while preserving the § 319.22 challenge to the court's authority to adjudicate title in an eviction.
- ACTIONS: Order the FLHSMV title/lien history via form HSMV 90510 (https://www.flhsmv.gov/pdf/forms/90510.pdf); watch for any post-July 9 re-titling attempt (strong § 319.33 evidence).
- URL: http://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0300-0399/0319/Sections/0319.22.html
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Title-application duties — dealer must obtain title in the purchaser's name
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 319.23(6) (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: In a sale by a licensed dealer, "the certificate of title must be obtained in the name of the purchaser by the dealer"; applications must be filed within 30 days after delivery; late-filing penalties apply.
- APPLIES: If the seller was (or acted for) a licensed mobile home dealer (§ 320.77), the DEALER owed the title-transfer duty — a statutory violation supporting the FLHSMV complaint. If not a dealer, Rodgers' inability to apply within 30 days flows from the seller's non-delivery — state that in the FLHSMV affidavit and criminal report.
- ACTIONS: Check whether the seller/Sweet Living holds a mobile home dealer license (§ 320.77).
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Title fraud — third-degree felony
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 319.33 (2025) [CORRECTED — the contraband/forfeiture clause covers 'motor vehicle' only, not mobile homes]
- SAYS: Prohibits altering/forging certificates, using forged titles, false statements in title applications and fraud in title documentation, using invalid/fictitious/stolen title documents to obtain money, and possession with intent to defraud. Third-degree felony.
- APPLIES: The title documented here shows a recorded sale price of $2,000 (vs. $18,000 paid) and a recorded date of Jan 2, 2026 (vs. the Dec 6, 2025 transaction) — false statements in title documentation squarely within § 319.33(1)(e). Cite alongside § 319.34 and § 812.014 in the reports and let investigators choose the charge.
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Selling without delivering an assigned certificate is itself a crime
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 319.34 (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: "Whoever... purports to sell or transfer a motor vehicle or mobile home without delivering to the purchaser or transferee thereof a certificate of title thereto duly assigned... shall be fined not more than $500 or imprisoned for not more than 6 months, or both, for each offense."
- APPLIES: The most directly on-point criminal statute: $18,000 paid (incl. $17,000 cash Dec 6, 2025, with an eyewitness), no assigned certificate ever delivered.
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Grand theft grading — THIRD degree, not second
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 812.014(2)(c)3. (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: Second-degree grand theft requires $20,000+; $10,000–$20,000 is grand theft of the third degree.
- APPLIES: ~$18,000 = 'grand theft in the third degree, § 812.014(2)(c)3.' — overstating the grade undermines credibility in every report and demand letter.
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Civil theft — treble damages; mandatory 30-day pre-suit demand; 5-year limitations
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 772.11, § 772.17 (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: Clear-and-convincing proof of a § 812.014 violation → "threefold the actual damages" + fees/costs. "Before filing an action... the person claiming injury must make a written demand for $200 or the treble damage amount"; compliance within 30 days of receipt earns a release for the specific act. Defendant recovers fees if the claim "was without substantial fact or legal support." 5-year limitations (tolled during related criminal proceedings + 2 years).
- APPLIES: Send the certified-mail treble demand (~$54,000) to the seller and Sweet Living now; the earliest suit date is 30 days after receipt. The fee-shifting clause means the civil-theft count needs the clearest intent evidence (the falsified title data helps); contract/FDUTPA counts carry no such risk.
- DEADLINE: Suit no earlier than 30 days after demand receipt; limitations ~5 years.
- URL: http://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0700-0799/0772/Sections/0772.11.html
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Civil paths: compel title vs. rescind-and-recover
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 319.28(2)(a); § 319.23; ch. 86 (declaratory judgments) [§ 319.28(2)(a) verified verbatim]
- SAYS: When ownership passes by court order, FLHSMV issues title on an affidavit plus the court order. BALS practice guidance: cooperative seller → HSMV 82050 + 82040 MH at the tax collector; unavailable/refusing seller → declaratory judgment directing title into the buyer's name.
- APPLIES: Track A — COMPEL TITLE: declaratory judgment/specific performance, then § 319.28(2)(a) at the tax collector. Track B — RESCIND AND RECOVER: breach of contract, conversion, FDUTPA, civil theft for the $18,000 (trebled if civil theft proven). She already rescinded by certified mail in Jan 2026 (signed by the seller) — which points toward Track B, but the tracks may be pleaded in the alternative. Forum note: an $18,000 claim fits county court (§ 34.01 cap $50,000); a $54,000 treble claim likely goes to circuit court.
- DEADLINE: Written contract ~5 years; oral ~4 years (§ 95.11 — verify subsections before filing).
- CONFIDENCE: high

## FDUTPA claim and AG complaint
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 501.204(1), § 501.211 (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- APPLIES: Taking $18,000 for a home while never conveying title, never securing park approval, then evicting the buyer is a textbook FDUTPA pattern for a company acting in trade or commerce. Actual damages + fees (§ 501.2105 — two-way fee risk). ~4-year limitations. Free AG complaint (myfloridalegal.com, 1-866-9-NO-SCAM) — pattern enforcement, not individual representation.
- CONFIDENCE: high

## Mobile home = tangible PERSONAL property on a rented lot — why it matters
- AUTHORITY: Fla. Stat. § 320.015(1), § 320.0815, § 723.002 (2025) [CONFIRMED]
- SAYS: A mobile home is real property only when the homeowner also owns the land and the home is permanently affixed; otherwise tangible personal property (MH decal, not RP).
- APPLIES: (1) Ownership of the home runs through the ch. 319 title system — the eviction judgment could adjudicate at most possession of the LOT, never title to the home; any judgment/writ language treating Sweet Living as the home's owner exceeds what an eviction can decide. (2) Recovery of the home itself would be replevin (ch. 78) or conversion damages. (3) Home owner + rented lot in a 10+ lot park → ch. 723 governs the lot tenancy (feeds the vehicle-mismatch theory). (4) Post-writ disposition of the home is a personal-property loss claim (conversion/civil theft) even though lot possession is gone — document the home's condition from the public road.
- CONFIDENCE: high
